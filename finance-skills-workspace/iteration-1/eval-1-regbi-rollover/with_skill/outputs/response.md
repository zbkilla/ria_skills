# Standard of Care and Documentation for a 401(k)-to-IRA Rollover at a Dual-Registrant Firm

## Which Standard Applies

Because you are a dual-registrant, the answer depends on the capacity in which you make the rollover recommendation:

- **If you are acting in your broker-dealer capacity:** SEC Regulation Best Interest (Reg BI, 17 CFR 240.15l-1) applies. Reg BI is a point-of-recommendation standard that requires you to act in the client's best interest at the time of the recommendation, but does not impose an ongoing monitoring obligation afterward.

- **If you are acting in your investment adviser capacity:** The full IA fiduciary duty under Advisers Act Sections 206(1) and 206(2) applies. This is a broader, ongoing standard that includes a continuous duty of care, a duty of loyalty, ongoing monitoring, and best execution obligations for the life of the relationship.

**You must clearly disclose to your client which capacity you are acting in before making this recommendation.** The SEC has specifically warned dual-registrants against "hat-switching" -- moving clients between BD and IA capacity without clear disclosure, potentially to exploit the less demanding standard. This capacity disclosure should be in writing and acknowledged by the client.

As a practical matter, if this client's IRA will be managed on an ongoing advisory basis (fee-based, with you providing continuous advice), you are almost certainly acting in your IA capacity and owe a fiduciary duty. If the IRA will be a self-directed brokerage account where you earn commissions on transactions, Reg BI applies.

## What Both Standards Require for This Rollover

Regardless of which hat you are wearing, a 401(k)-to-IRA rollover recommendation triggers substantial obligations. The SEC Staff Bulletin on Account Recommendations (2022) specifically addresses rollovers and requires you to evaluate and document four alternatives:

1. **Stay in the current 401(k) plan** -- this is a "reasonably available alternative" that must be considered
2. **Roll to a new employer's plan** (if the client has one or will have one)
3. **Roll to an IRA at your firm** (your recommendation)
4. **Take a cash distribution** (and pay taxes/penalties)

For each alternative, you must compare:

- **Investment options:** What funds/investments are available in the current 401(k) vs. what you will offer in the IRA? Many 401(k) plans offer institutional share classes with expense ratios of 0.02-0.10% that may not be available in a retail IRA.
- **Fees and expenses:** Plan-level administrative fees, fund expense ratios, advisory or wrap fees, transaction costs, custodial fees. On $340K, even a 0.30% fee differential means roughly $1,020/year in additional cost.
- **Services:** Does the 401(k) offer investment guidance, managed account options, or financial planning? What services will you provide through the IRA?
- **Penalty-free withdrawal access:** This is particularly important for your client. At age 58, your client is not yet 59-1/2. Under the 401(k) separation-from-service rule, if your client has separated or separates from the employer at age 55 or later, they can take penalty-free withdrawals from the 401(k). If they roll to an IRA, they cannot take penalty-free withdrawals until age 59-1/2 (unless they use a 72(t) substantially equal periodic payment plan or qualify under another exception). This is a material difference that could cost the client a 10% early withdrawal penalty on any distributions taken before 59-1/2.
- **Creditor protection:** The 401(k) has broad ERISA creditor protection (federal). IRA creditor protection varies by state and is generally narrower.
- **Required minimum distributions:** Not immediately relevant at 58, but RMD rules differ slightly (still-working exception for 401(k) does not apply to IRAs).

## Documentation You Need to Keep

### Under Reg BI (broker-dealer capacity):

1. **Form CRS delivery record** -- confirm the dual-registrant Form CRS was delivered before or at the time of the recommendation
2. **Capacity disclosure** -- written record that you disclosed you are acting in your BD capacity, and what that means vs. advisory capacity
3. **Client investment profile** -- age (58), financial situation, other investments, tax status, investment objectives, time horizon, liquidity needs, risk tolerance, investment experience
4. **Rollover alternatives analysis** -- documented comparison of the four alternatives (stay in plan, roll to new employer plan, roll to IRA, cash distribution) with specific attention to fees, investment options, services, the age-55 separation-from-service withdrawal rule, and creditor protections
5. **Reasonably available alternatives on your product shelf** -- if recommending specific investments in the IRA, document why those were chosen over other available options, particularly if lower-cost alternatives exist
6. **Conflict of interest disclosure** -- how you and the firm are compensated on the IRA assets (commissions, 12b-1 fees, revenue sharing, advisory fees), and how those conflicts are mitigated
7. **Basis for recommendation** -- a written narrative explaining why the rollover to your firm's IRA is in this client's best interest despite any cost differentials or loss of 401(k)-specific features

### Under IA fiduciary duty (adviser capacity):

All of the above, plus:

8. **Full and fair conflict disclosure with informed consent** -- not just disclosure, but evidence that the client understood the conflicts and consented (the fiduciary standard requires informed consent, not just disclosure)
9. **Ongoing monitoring plan** -- document the scope and frequency of ongoing portfolio monitoring
10. **Fee reasonableness documentation** -- demonstrate that the advisory fee is reasonable relative to services provided
11. **ADV Part 2A and 2B delivery** -- confirm delivery of the firm's brochure and brochure supplement

### If ERISA/DOL Considerations Apply:

If you are providing advice that makes you a fiduciary under the DOL's rules (which rollover recommendations can trigger), you may also need to comply with **PTE 2020-02** conditions if you are receiving compensation that would otherwise be a prohibited transaction (commissions, 12b-1 fees, revenue sharing). PTE 2020-02 requires: acting in the customer's best interest, providing balanced disclosure, charging only reasonable compensation, adopting anti-conflict policies, and conducting retrospective compliance reviews.

## Key Risk Flag for This Client

At age 58, the loss of the 401(k) age-55 separation-from-service penalty-free withdrawal provision is a significant factor. If your client has separated or plans to separate from their employer and may need access to funds before 59-1/2, recommending a rollover could cost them a 10% early withdrawal penalty on any distributions. You must document that you considered this and explain why the rollover is still in the client's best interest despite this disadvantage, or alternatively, recommend that the client delay the rollover until after reaching 59-1/2.

## Summary

| Element | What to Do |
|---------|-----------|
| Capacity disclosure | Clearly tell the client (in writing) whether you are acting as broker or adviser |
| Standard that applies | Reg BI if acting as BD; fiduciary duty if acting as IA |
| Alternatives analysis | Document comparison of stay-in-plan, roll to new employer plan, roll to IRA, cash distribution |
| Fee comparison | Quantify fee differential between 401(k) and proposed IRA |
| Age-55 rule | Flag that rollover eliminates penalty-free access before 59-1/2 |
| Creditor protection | Note that ERISA protection is lost upon rollover to IRA |
| Conflict disclosure | Disclose all compensation the firm and you receive on IRA assets |
| Recommendation basis | Written explanation of why the IRA is in the client's best interest |
| Form CRS | Deliver the dual-registrant Form CRS before or at time of recommendation |
