# Rollover Recommendation: Standard of Care and Documentation Requirements

## Which Standard Applies

As a dual-registrant, the standard of care depends on the **capacity** in which you are acting when making the rollover recommendation:

### If Acting in Broker-Dealer Capacity: Regulation Best Interest (Reg BI)

If you are recommending the rollover in your capacity as a registered representative (broker), **SEC Regulation Best Interest** applies. Reg BI became effective June 30, 2020, and requires that you act in the **best interest** of the retail customer at the time the recommendation is made, without placing your financial or other interest ahead of the customer's interest.

Reg BI has four component obligations:

1. **Disclosure Obligation** -- You must provide the customer with a **Form CRS (Customer Relationship Summary)** and full disclosure of all material facts about the scope and terms of the relationship, including fees, costs, conflicts of interest, and the type and scope of services.

2. **Care Obligation** -- You must exercise reasonable diligence, care, and skill to:
   - Understand the potential risks, rewards, and costs associated with the rollover recommendation
   - Have a reasonable basis to believe the rollover is in the customer's best interest (considering the customer's investment profile and the potential risks, rewards, and costs)
   - Have a reasonable basis to believe the recommendation is in the customer's best interest and does not place your interest ahead of theirs

3. **Conflict of Interest Obligation** -- You must establish, maintain, and enforce written policies and procedures to identify, disclose, and at a minimum mitigate (or in certain cases eliminate) conflicts of interest associated with the recommendation.

4. **Compliance Obligation** -- You must establish, maintain, and enforce written policies and procedures reasonably designed to achieve compliance with Reg BI as a whole.

**For rollovers specifically**, Reg BI requires you to consider the 401(k) plan as an alternative -- that is, you must evaluate whether staying in the plan is actually the better option. You cannot simply assume rolling into an IRA is better.

### If Acting in Investment Adviser Capacity: Fiduciary Duty

If the IRA account will be an advisory account (fee-based, discretionary or non-discretionary advisory), you are acting as an **investment adviser** and owe a **fiduciary duty** under the Investment Advisers Act of 1940. This is a principles-based standard requiring:

- **Duty of Care** -- Provide advice that is in the client's best interest, including a reasonable investigation into the client's financial situation, objectives, and needs.
- **Duty of Loyalty** -- Not place your own interests ahead of the client's. Full and fair disclosure of all material conflicts of interest.

The fiduciary standard is generally considered more stringent than Reg BI, though in practice the analysis for a rollover is similar.

### Practical Reality for This Situation

Since you are recommending the client roll into an IRA **with your firm**, you need to determine upfront which capacity the receiving account will be in. If it will be a brokerage IRA, Reg BI applies. If it will be an advisory IRA, fiduciary duty applies. **Either way, you must document a thorough analysis of why rolling over is in the client's best interest compared to leaving the assets in the 401(k).**

## Documentation You Need

Regardless of which standard applies, robust documentation is critical. For a $340K rollover with a 58-year-old client, you should document:

### 1. Account and Client Information
- Client's age (58), retirement timeline, income, tax bracket, other assets, risk tolerance, and investment objectives
- Current 401(k) plan details: plan name, custodian, investment options available, expense ratios, any employer match still vesting
- Whether the client is still employed with the 401(k) plan sponsor or has separated from service

### 2. Comparative Analysis (the "Reasons Why" documentation)
This is the most important piece. Document a side-by-side comparison of:

- **Fees and expenses**: Compare 401(k) plan investment expenses and administrative fees vs. projected IRA fees (advisory fees, transaction costs, fund expense ratios). For $340K, even a 0.25% fee difference is meaningful over time.
- **Investment options**: Compare available investments in the plan vs. what will be available in the IRA
- **Services**: What additional services (financial planning, tax management, rebalancing) does the IRA provide that the plan does not?
- **Penalty-free access**: At 58, the client may be able to take penalty-free distributions from the 401(k) under the "separation from service at age 55 or later" rule (IRC Section 72(t)(2)(A)(v)). Rolling to an IRA would lose this -- IRA withdrawals before 59 1/2 generally incur a 10% early withdrawal penalty. **This is a critical factor to document and discuss.**
- **Creditor protection**: 401(k) plans have federal ERISA protection from creditors. IRA creditor protection varies by state.
- **Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)**: Both are subject to RMDs, but if the client is still working, the 401(k) may allow deferral of RMDs past age 73 (still-working exception).
- **Roth conversion opportunities**: If the client is considering Roth conversions, an IRA rollover may facilitate that strategy.
- **Net unrealized appreciation (NUA)**: If the 401(k) holds appreciated employer stock, NUA treatment could provide significant tax benefits that would be lost in a rollover.

### 3. Conflict of Interest Disclosure
- Document your compensation from the rollover (commissions, advisory fees, revenue sharing)
- Compare to what you would earn if the client stayed in the plan (likely nothing)
- Document how this conflict was disclosed and mitigated

### 4. Form CRS Delivery (if Reg BI applies)
- Document that Form CRS was provided to the client before or at the time of the recommendation

### 5. Client Communication Records
- Notes from conversations with the client about the rollover
- Any written communications (emails, letters) discussing the recommendation
- Evidence the client understood the trade-offs

### 6. Signed Rollover Documentation
- IRA application and rollover paperwork
- Any acknowledgment forms your firm requires for rollover recommendations

## Key Risk Flags for This Specific Client

Given the specifics you mentioned, pay particular attention to:

1. **Age 58 and the Rule of 55**: If this client has separated from service (or will soon), they can take penalty-free withdrawals from the 401(k) starting at age 55. Rolling to an IRA eliminates this option -- they would need to wait until 59 1/2 for penalty-free IRA withdrawals (or use 72(t) substantially equal periodic payments, which are inflexible). If the client might need access to funds before 59 1/2, this is a significant consideration that must be documented and discussed.

2. **Fee comparison at $340K**: At this account size, the fee differential matters. If the 401(k) has institutional share classes at 0.03-0.10% and your IRA will charge a 1.0% advisory fee plus retail fund expenses, the cost difference is substantial and must be justified by additional value.

3. **Dual-registrant conflicts**: FINRA and the SEC have specifically flagged dual-registrant rollovers as a high-conflict area. Your firm's compliance department likely has specific procedures for documenting rollover recommendations -- follow them carefully.

## Regulatory References

- **SEC Regulation Best Interest**: 17 CFR 240.15l-1 (adopted June 2019, effective June 2020)
- **SEC Staff Bulletin on Account Recommendations** (particularly regarding rollovers): Staff Bulletin on Standards of Conduct for Broker-Dealers and Investment Advisers -- Account Recommendations for Retail Investors
- **FINRA Regulatory Notice 13-45**: Rollover guidance (predates Reg BI but still relevant)
- **Investment Advisers Act of 1940, Sections 206(1) and 206(2)**: Anti-fraud / fiduciary duty provisions
- **IRC Section 72(t)(2)(A)(v)**: Separation from service at age 55+ penalty-free withdrawal exception
- **DOL PTE 2020-02**: If any retirement plan assets are involved and you are receiving compensation, the DOL's prohibited transaction exemption may also apply, requiring additional documentation that the advice is in the retirement investor's best interest

---

## Bottom Line

For a dual-registrant handling a $340K rollover for a 58-year-old, you must: (1) determine which capacity you are acting in, (2) apply the corresponding standard (Reg BI or fiduciary), (3) conduct and document a thorough comparative analysis that specifically addresses the Rule of 55 issue given the client's age, fee differentials, and your compensation conflict, and (4) retain all documentation in the client file. When in doubt, apply the higher fiduciary standard -- it protects both the client and you.
