# Fiduciary Analysis: Proprietary Fund Recommendation

## Short Answer

Yes, this is a fiduciary issue. As a registered investment adviser, your firm owes a fiduciary duty of loyalty under IA Act Sections 206(1) and 206(2), which prohibits placing the firm's financial interests ahead of the client's. Recommending a proprietary fund when a cheaper third-party alternative with similar performance exists is a textbook conflict of interest that triggers heightened obligations under both the duty of loyalty and the duty of care.

## Why This Is a Problem

### Duty of Loyalty Violation Risk

The SEC's 2019 Fiduciary Interpretation (Release IA-5248) is explicit: an adviser must not place its own interests ahead of the client's interests. Recommending a proprietary fund generates revenue for the firm at both the advisory-fee level and the product-fee level -- a layered conflict. The duty of loyalty requires:

1. **Full and fair disclosure** of all material conflicts, and that disclosure must be **sufficiently specific** that the client can understand the conflict and provide informed consent. Generic language such as "we may recommend affiliated products" in the ADV Part 2A is insufficient under the SEC's standard.
2. **Informed client consent** after meaningful disclosure -- not blanket advance consent buried in onboarding paperwork.
3. Even with proper disclosure and consent, the adviser **still cannot act in a manner inconsistent with the client's best interest**. Disclosure does not cure a bad recommendation.

### Duty of Care Concern

The duty of care requires the adviser to have a reasonable understanding of the client's objectives and provide advice in the client's best interest in light of those objectives. For a retired teacher with moderate risk tolerance and a $600K portfolio, cost efficiency is material. Every basis point of unnecessary expense directly reduces retirement income. If the proprietary fund charges more than the comparable third-party option and does not deliver demonstrably superior net-of-fee performance, the adviser has not met the duty of care standard.

### Proprietary Product Conflicts Under SEC Enforcement Precedent

SEC enforcement actions have specifically targeted firms that failed to adequately disclose preferences for proprietary products, particularly where lower-cost third-party alternatives were available. The SEC's 2018-2019 share-class selection initiative resulted in significant penalties and disgorgement orders against firms engaged in exactly this pattern. The Commission has made clear that recommending proprietary or affiliated products creates a direct financial conflict because the firm earns revenue from both the advisory fee and the product-level fee.

## What the Firm Must Demonstrate

To defend this recommendation, the firm needs to show all of the following:

1. **Specific disclosure**: The client received disclosure that specifically identifies (a) the financial benefit the firm receives from the proprietary fund, (b) the cost differential between the proprietary fund and the comparable third-party alternative, and (c) the percentage of the client's assets placed in proprietary products. Boilerplate ADV language does not satisfy this requirement.

2. **Informed consent**: The client understood the conflict and affirmatively consented after receiving the specific disclosure described above.

3. **Best interest justification**: Documentation showing why the proprietary fund is in this specific client's best interest despite the higher cost. This could include superior risk-adjusted performance net of fees, specific features or exposures not available in the third-party alternative, or other tangible client benefits. "Similar performance" at a higher cost is very difficult to justify.

4. **Suitability for this client**: For a retired client living on portfolio income, cost drag is especially harmful because it compounds over a potentially long retirement horizon. The adviser must document why the cost differential is justified for this client's specific circumstances.

## The Mitigation Hierarchy

Regulatory expectations for addressing conflicts follow a clear hierarchy:

1. **Eliminate**: Stop recommending the proprietary fund when a cheaper comparable exists. This is the cleanest resolution.
2. **Mitigate**: If the firm continues to offer proprietary products, implement structural safeguards -- independent review committees, compliance pre-approval for proprietary recommendations, compensation leveling so the adviser does not earn more for recommending proprietary products, and caps on proprietary product allocation.
3. **Disclose**: At minimum, provide the specific, quantified disclosure described above. But disclosure alone is not sufficient under fiduciary duty for conflicts that can reasonably be eliminated or mitigated. An adviser cannot "disclose away" a conflict.

## Recommended Immediate Steps

1. **Review the recommendation**: Determine the specific cost differential and whether the proprietary fund offers any demonstrable advantage that justifies the higher expense for this client.
2. **Check disclosure adequacy**: Review the firm's ADV Part 2A and any client-facing disclosures to determine whether the proprietary product conflict is disclosed with sufficient specificity (not just boilerplate).
3. **Document the rationale**: If the recommendation stands, create contemporaneous documentation of why the proprietary fund is in this retired client's best interest despite the cost differential.
4. **Consider reversal**: If the firm cannot document a best-interest justification, the adviser should recommend switching the client to the lower-cost third-party alternative and the firm should consider whether client restitution is appropriate for any excess fees already charged.
5. **Review firm-wide practices**: Determine whether this is an isolated recommendation or a pattern. If advisers are systematically favoring proprietary funds, the firm has a systemic compliance issue that requires policy changes, not just case-by-case remediation.
6. **Assess compensation structures**: If the adviser receives higher compensation for recommending proprietary products, the firm must either level the compensation or implement enhanced supervisory review for all proprietary recommendations.

## Applicable Standards and Rules

- **IA Act Sections 206(1) and 206(2)** -- anti-fraud provisions establishing the federal fiduciary duty
- **SEC 2019 Fiduciary Interpretation (Release IA-5248)** -- duty of care and duty of loyalty framework
- **SEC Rule 206(4)-7** -- requirement for written compliance policies and annual compliance review
- **SEC share-class selection enforcement actions (2018-2019)** -- precedent for proprietary product conflict enforcement

## Bottom Line

For an RIA, recommending a more expensive proprietary fund over a comparable cheaper alternative to a retired client with a moderate portfolio is a high-risk fiduciary issue. The burden is on the firm to demonstrate that the recommendation is in the client's best interest despite the conflict -- not merely suitable, not merely disclosed, but affirmatively in the client's best interest. If the firm cannot make that showing with specific documentation, the recommendation should be reversed and the firm's proprietary product policies should be reviewed.
