# Fiduciary Analysis: Proprietary Fund Recommendation

## Short Answer

Yes, this raises a significant fiduciary concern. As a registered investment adviser (RIA), your firm and its advisers owe clients a fiduciary duty under the Investment Advisers Act of 1940. Recommending a proprietary fund over a cheaper third-party alternative with comparable performance, without adequate justification and disclosure, is a potential breach of that duty.

## The Fiduciary Standard for RIAs

RIAs are held to a fiduciary standard established under the Investment Advisers Act of 1940 and reinforced by SEC interpretive guidance (notably the June 2019 Commission Interpretation Regarding Standard of Conduct for Investment Advisers). This standard has two core components:

1. **Duty of Care** -- The adviser must provide advice that is in the client's best interest, including a reasonable investigation into the recommendation. This means evaluating cost, performance, risk, and suitability relative to available alternatives.

2. **Duty of Loyalty** -- The adviser must not place their own interests (or the firm's interests) ahead of the client's. Revenue derived from proprietary products creates a conflict of interest that must be managed and disclosed.

## Why This Situation Is Problematic

### Cost Drag on a Retirement Portfolio

For a retired client with a $600K moderate-risk portfolio, expense ratios matter enormously. If the proprietary fund charges, say, 0.75% versus 0.15% for a comparable third-party index or actively managed fund, that is roughly $3,600 per year in additional fees -- money directly reducing the client's retirement income or portfolio longevity. Over a 20-year retirement horizon, compounded cost drag from that difference could reduce the portfolio by $70,000-$100,000 or more depending on returns.

A retired teacher likely has a relatively fixed income (pension plus Social Security plus portfolio withdrawals). Every basis point of unnecessary cost erodes purchasing power in a way that cannot be recovered.

### Conflict of Interest

Proprietary fund recommendations inherently create a conflict because the adviser's firm earns revenue (management fees, distribution fees, or both) from the product. The SEC has been clear that this type of conflict must be:

- **Identified** by the firm's compliance program
- **Disclosed** to the client in plain language (typically via Form ADV Part 2A)
- **Mitigated** through policies that ensure the recommendation still serves the client's best interest despite the conflict

If the adviser cannot articulate a legitimate, client-centered reason for the proprietary fund -- such as meaningfully different investment strategy, tax-loss harvesting opportunity, access to an asset class unavailable elsewhere, or demonstrably superior risk-adjusted returns -- then the recommendation appears to serve the firm's economic interest rather than the client's.

### SEC and Examination Priorities

The SEC Division of Examinations has repeatedly flagged proprietary product recommendations as a priority area. Enforcement actions have targeted RIAs that:

- Failed to disclose the conflict adequately in Form ADV
- Did not conduct a best-interest analysis comparing proprietary and third-party options
- Lacked written policies governing proprietary product recommendations
- Created compensation structures that incentivized advisers to recommend proprietary products

The SEC's share class initiative and subsequent enforcement sweep (2018-2020) resulted in over $100 million in disgorgement from advisers who recommended higher-cost proprietary or affiliated share classes without adequate disclosure.

## What the Firm Should Do Now

### Immediate Steps

1. **Review the recommendation** -- Have the CCO or a senior adviser independently assess whether the proprietary fund is genuinely in the client's best interest given her profile (retired, moderate risk, $600K). Document the analysis comparing the proprietary fund against the third-party alternative on fees, performance, risk characteristics, tax efficiency, and any other relevant factors.

2. **Assess disclosure** -- Confirm that the firm's Form ADV Part 2A adequately discloses the conflict of interest related to proprietary products. Verify that the client received and acknowledged this disclosure.

3. **Talk to the adviser** -- Understand the rationale. If the adviser has a legitimate, documented reason for the recommendation, that changes the analysis. If they do not, this needs to be addressed as a supervisory matter.

4. **Consider unwinding** -- If the proprietary fund cannot be justified on its merits, the client should be moved to the lower-cost alternative. Depending on tax implications and any redemption fees, the firm may need to make the client whole for any excess costs incurred.

### Systemic Steps

5. **Review policies and procedures** -- Ensure the firm has a written policy on proprietary product recommendations that requires documented best-interest analysis.

6. **Examine compensation structures** -- If the adviser receives higher compensation for proprietary fund sales, that exacerbates the conflict and may need to be restructured.

7. **Audit other clients** -- If one adviser made this recommendation, others may have as well. Conduct a broader review of proprietary fund placements across the book of business.

## Key Regulatory References

- **Investment Advisers Act of 1940, Sections 206(1) and 206(2)** -- Anti-fraud provisions establishing fiduciary duty
- **SEC Commission Interpretation Regarding Standard of Conduct for Investment Advisers (June 2019)** -- Clarifies duty of care and duty of loyalty
- **SEC Form ADV Part 2A, Item 10** -- Requires disclosure of material conflicts including proprietary products
- **SEC Risk Alert: Observations from Examinations of Investment Advisers (November 2020)** -- Highlights deficiencies in conflict-of-interest disclosures related to proprietary products

## Bottom Line

An RIA recommending a more expensive proprietary fund to a retired client on a moderate fixed portfolio, when a cheaper comparable option exists, faces a real fiduciary risk. The burden is on the adviser and firm to demonstrate that the recommendation serves the client's interest -- not the firm's revenue. Without a compelling, documented justification, this looks like a duty-of-loyalty violation that the SEC would scrutinize. Address it proactively: review the recommendation, ensure proper disclosure, and if warranted, remediate the client's position.
