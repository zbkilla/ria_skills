# Fee Disclosure Guidance for Form ADV Part 2A

Your situation involves three distinct compensation streams that must each be disclosed under Item 5 of Form ADV Part 2A: a tiered AUM fee, a fixed financial planning fee, and 12b-1 fee revenue from mutual fund positions. Below is how to handle each, followed by the conflict-of-interest disclosure that ties them together.

---

## 1. Tiered AUM Fee Schedule

Disclose the full fee schedule with explicit tiers, billing method, and minimum account size:

| Assets Under Management | Annual Fee Rate |
|---|---|
| First $1,000,000 | 1.00% |
| Next $2,000,000 ($1M -- $3M) | 0.75% |
| Above $3,000,000 | 0.50% |

For each tier, specify:

- **Billing frequency** -- whether you bill monthly, quarterly, or another interval.
- **In advance or in arrears** -- state which applies. If in advance, you must also disclose your refund policy for prepaid fees if the relationship terminates mid-period.
- **Pro-ration** -- describe how partial-period fees are calculated (e.g., for new accounts opened mid-quarter or accounts that terminate mid-billing cycle).
- **Fee calculation basis** -- whether the fee is calculated on the market value of assets at the beginning of the billing period, end of period, or a daily average.
- **Household or account-level tiering** -- clarify whether the tiers apply per account or across related accounts (householding), as this affects the effective rate.

**Example dollar disclosure (recommended but not required):** A client with $2,500,000 in AUM would pay $10,000 on the first $1M plus $11,250 on the next $1.5M, totaling $21,250 annually (an effective rate of 0.85%), before accounting for fund-level expenses.

## 2. Fixed Financial Planning Fee

Disclose the $2,500 annual financial planning fee separately from the AUM fee:

- State that the fee is $2,500 per year, whether it is billed as a lump sum or in installments, and whether it is billed in advance or arrears.
- Describe what services the financial planning fee covers and whether it is optional or required.
- Explain whether the fee is negotiable or if there are circumstances under which it may be waived or reduced.
- If the fee is billed in advance, disclose the refund policy if the client terminates before the planning period ends.
- Clarify whether the financial planning fee applies in addition to the AUM fee or whether clients can engage for financial planning only.

## 3. 12b-1 Fee Revenue -- This Is the Critical Disclosure

This is the area where the SEC has brought the most enforcement actions related to fee disclosure. Receiving 12b-1 fees from mutual fund positions while also charging an advisory fee requires specific, prominent disclosure. You must address all of the following:

### a. Disclose that you receive 12b-1 fees
State explicitly that the firm (and/or its supervised persons) receives 12b-1 distribution fees from certain mutual funds held in client accounts. Do not bury this in general language like "some funds may charge distribution fees." Name the compensation stream directly.

### b. Quantify or estimate the amount
Provide clients with enough information to understand the magnitude. For example: "12b-1 fees received by our firm typically range from 0.25% to 0.50% of assets invested in the applicable mutual fund share classes." If you can provide a dollar estimate or a range based on a typical account, do so.

### c. Disclose the total cost to the client
The client's total cost is not just your advisory fee. It is the advisory fee plus the fund-level expenses (including the 12b-1 fee). Make this explicit. For example: "A client with $1,000,000 invested in mutual funds with an average expense ratio of 0.60% (including a 0.25% 12b-1 fee) would bear a total annual cost of approximately 1.60% -- the 1.00% advisory fee plus the 0.60% fund expense ratio."

### d. Disclose the conflict of interest
State that receiving 12b-1 fee revenue creates a conflict of interest because it gives the firm a financial incentive to recommend mutual fund share classes that pay 12b-1 fees over share classes of the same fund that do not (such as institutional shares or no-load shares). This is required under both Item 5 and Item 10 (Other Financial Industry Activities and Affiliations) of the ADV Part 2A.

### e. Address share class selection
Describe your firm's policy for selecting share classes. If lower-cost share classes (institutional, retirement, or clean shares) are available for the same fund and the client meets the investment minimum, explain how you determine which share class to use. The SEC's Share Class Selection Disclosure Initiative (2018) resulted in over $139 million in disgorgement from firms that failed to disclose 12b-1 revenue and did not place clients in the lowest-cost share class available to them.

### f. Describe how you mitigate the conflict
Explain what steps the firm takes to address this conflict. Common approaches include:
- Offsetting 12b-1 fees against the advisory fee (fee offset)
- Using institutional or no-load share classes where available
- Periodic review of share class appropriateness
- Crediting 12b-1 revenue back to client accounts

If you do not offset 12b-1 fees against your advisory fee, you must be especially clear that the client is bearing both costs.

## 4. Other Fees and Expenses

Item 5 also requires disclosure of other costs the client will bear beyond your advisory and planning fees:

- Custodian fees (account maintenance, wire fees, check fees)
- Mutual fund and ETF expense ratios (beyond the 12b-1 component)
- Transaction costs (if any trades incur commissions or ticket charges)
- Any other charges the client may incur

Include a statement that your fees are separate from and in addition to these other costs.

## 5. Form CRS Alignment

If you file a Form CRS (which you must if you are an SEC-registered or state-registered investment adviser), the "What are your fees?" section must summarize all three fee types. Form CRS is limited to two pages, so the summary will be brief, but it must include:

- The principal fees (AUM fee and planning fee)
- A statement that clients will also bear fund-level expenses including 12b-1 fees
- The required conversation starter: "Help me understand how these fees and costs might affect my investments. If I give you $10,000 to invest, how much will go to fees and costs, and how much will be invested for me?"
- A direction to the ADV Part 2A for the complete fee schedule

---

## Key Compliance Risks to Watch

1. **Do not describe your fund selection as "objective" or "independent"** if you receive 12b-1 fees from the funds you recommend. The SEC has brought enforcement actions against firms that used this language while receiving undisclosed revenue from recommended funds.

2. **Evaluate share class selection for every client.** If an institutional share class without 12b-1 fees is available and the client qualifies (based on account size or platform availability), you need a documented rationale for recommending a share class that pays 12b-1 fees.

3. **Update disclosures when compensation arrangements change.** If you add new fund families that pay 12b-1 fees, change your fee schedule, or enter into revenue sharing arrangements, the ADV must be updated promptly (and within 90 days of fiscal year-end for the annual amendment at a minimum).

4. **Do not assume "commission-free" means "cost-free."** If clients hold funds on a no-transaction-fee (NTF) platform, those funds may have higher expense ratios or 12b-1 fees that subsidize the "free" trading. Disclose this dynamic.
