# Form ADV Part 2A Fee Disclosure Guidance

## Overview

Form ADV Part 2A (the "Brochure") requires clear, complete disclosure of all compensation arrangements under Item 5 — Fees and Compensation. The SEC expects this section to be written in plain English so clients can understand the total cost of your services. For your firm, three distinct compensation streams must be disclosed: tiered AUM fees, a fixed financial planning fee, and 12b-1 fees received from mutual fund companies.

---

## 1. Tiered AUM Fee Schedule

### What to Disclose

Present the fee schedule in a clear table format. The SEC and state regulators expect investors to be able to calculate their approximate fee from the disclosure.

**Example disclosure language:**

> Our investment management fee is based on the market value of assets under management and is calculated using the following tiered schedule:
>
> | Assets Under Management | Annual Fee Rate |
> |---|---|
> | First $1,000,000 | 1.00% |
> | Next $2,000,000 ($1,000,001 -- $3,000,000) | 0.75% |
> | Above $3,000,000 | 0.50% |
>
> Fees are tiered (blended), meaning each tier's rate applies only to assets within that tier, not to the entire account value. For example, a $4,000,000 account would pay $10,000 (1.00% on the first $1M) + $15,000 (0.75% on the next $2M) + $5,000 (0.50% on the remaining $1M) = $30,000 annually, which equates to a blended rate of 0.75%.

### Additional Required Disclosures for AUM Fees

- **Billing frequency and method**: State whether fees are billed quarterly in advance, quarterly in arrears, or monthly. Specify whether fees are deducted directly from client accounts or invoiced.
- **Valuation date**: Explain what date or value is used to calculate the fee (e.g., market value on the last business day of the prior quarter).
- **Prorating**: Describe how fees are handled for new accounts opened or accounts closed mid-billing period.
- **Account aggregation (householding)**: If you allow related accounts to be combined for breakpoint purposes, disclose this. If you do not, also disclose that.
- **Fee negotiability**: If fees are negotiable or if certain clients (e.g., employees, family members, legacy clients) pay different rates, state this clearly and note that fee negotiation may create a conflict of interest.
- **Minimum account size**: If applicable, disclose any minimum asset requirement and whether it is waivable.

## 2. Fixed Financial Planning Fee

### What to Disclose

> In addition to investment management fees, we charge a fixed annual financial planning fee of $2,500. This fee covers [describe scope — e.g., comprehensive financial planning services including retirement planning, tax planning, estate planning, insurance review, and cash flow analysis].
>
> The financial planning fee is billed [quarterly at $625 per quarter / annually / etc.] and is [deducted from your investment account / invoiced separately]. This fee is separate from and in addition to the investment management fee described above.

### Additional Considerations

- **Scope of services**: Clearly define what the $2,500 covers so clients understand the value and do not confuse it with the AUM fee.
- **Termination and refunds**: Explain whether any portion of the fee is refundable if the client terminates mid-period.
- **Bundled vs. separate**: Make clear that a client could engage you for financial planning only, investment management only, or both, if that is the case. If the planning fee is mandatory for all AUM clients, disclose that.

## 3. 12b-1 Fee Disclosure (The Critical Conflict-of-Interest Section)

This is the area that draws the most regulatory scrutiny. The SEC's Share Class Selection Disclosure Initiative (2018-2019) resulted in over 100 enforcement actions against advisers who failed to properly disclose 12b-1 fee conflicts. You must be thorough here.

### What to Disclose

> Certain mutual funds in which we invest client assets pay us 12b-1 fees (also known as trail commissions or distribution fees). These fees are paid by the mutual fund company out of fund assets, meaning they are indirectly borne by you as a fund shareholder through higher fund expense ratios.
>
> **Conflict of interest**: The receipt of 12b-1 fees creates a conflict of interest because we have a financial incentive to recommend or retain mutual fund share classes that pay 12b-1 fees over share classes of the same fund that do not pay such fees (or that pay lower fees) but may have lower overall costs to you. For example, many mutual funds offer "institutional" or "advisor" share classes with lower expense ratios and no 12b-1 fees. Where a client is eligible for such a share class, we have a conflict in recommending a share class that pays us a 12b-1 fee.

### How You Mitigate the Conflict

You must also disclose how you address this conflict. Common approaches:

- **Offset arrangement**: "We offset 12b-1 fees received against the investment management fee, so clients are not paying twice for the same service." (If you do this, say so. If you do not, do not claim you do.)
- **Best share class policy**: "We have adopted a policy to invest clients in the lowest-cost share class for which they are eligible. Where an institutional share class is available, we will use it in preference to a share class that pays 12b-1 fees."
- **Full transparency**: "We receive approximately $[X] annually in 12b-1 fees. Clients may request a full accounting of 12b-1 fees received in connection with their accounts."

If you do NOT offset 12b-1 fees against your AUM fee, you must clearly disclose that the client is effectively paying both the AUM fee and the embedded 12b-1 fee, and that this represents additional compensation to the firm.

### Regulatory References

- **SEC Rule 206(4)-7** (Compliance Rule): Requires policies and procedures to address conflicts.
- **SEC Interpretation IA-5248** (2019, Fiduciary Interpretation): Reaffirmed that an adviser must make full and fair disclosure of all material conflicts of interest, including those arising from compensation arrangements.
- **ADV Part 2A, Item 5 Instructions**: Specifically require disclosure of compensation received from third parties in connection with client investments.
- **ADV Part 2A, Item 12 (Brokerage Practices)**: May also be relevant if 12b-1 fees influence fund selection.

## 4. Bringing It All Together: Total Cost Transparency

Consider including a "total cost" example that helps clients understand the all-in cost of your services:

> **Illustrative total annual cost for a $2,000,000 account:**
>
> | Fee Component | Amount |
> |---|---|
> | Investment management fee (blended) | $17,500 |
> | Financial planning fee | $2,500 |
> | **Total advisory fees paid to our firm** | **$20,000** |
>
> In addition to the fees above, clients bear the internal expenses of the mutual funds and ETFs held in their accounts (expense ratios), as well as any brokerage transaction costs. Some mutual fund positions may pay 12b-1 fees to our firm as described above. These costs are not included in the total above but affect your net investment returns.

## 5. Other Item 5 Requirements to Address

Beyond your three fee types, Item 5 also requires you to disclose:

- **Other fees clients will pay**: Custodian fees, transaction/trading costs, wire fees, account maintenance fees, and fund-level expenses (expense ratios). Make clear that these are separate from your advisory fee.
- **Whether your firm or supervised persons receive compensation for the sale of securities or insurance products**: If any associated persons are licensed insurance agents or registered representatives of a broker-dealer, this must be disclosed as a conflict.
- **Prepayment of fees**: If fees are collected in advance, clients need to know their right to a refund of prepaid fees upon termination.

---

## Key Drafting Principles

1. **Plain English**: Avoid jargon. If you must use a term like "12b-1 fee," define it immediately.
2. **Specificity over vagueness**: "Our fees range from 0.50% to 1.00%" with a clear schedule is better than "fees are negotiable based on account size."
3. **Conflicts front and center**: Do not bury the 12b-1 conflict disclosure. Regulators look for this specifically.
4. **Consistency with other documents**: Ensure the fee schedule matches your advisory agreement, client onboarding documents, and any fee schedule posted on your website.
5. **Annual review**: Update Item 5 whenever fees change and as part of your annual ADV amendment filing.

---

## Compliance Review Checklist

Before finalizing Item 5:

- [ ] Fee schedule is presented in a clear, tabular format with worked examples
- [ ] Billing method, frequency, and valuation date are specified
- [ ] Proration and termination/refund policies are stated
- [ ] Financial planning fee is described separately with scope of services
- [ ] 12b-1 fee receipt is disclosed with explicit conflict-of-interest language
- [ ] Mitigation of the 12b-1 conflict is described (offset, best share class policy, or neither)
- [ ] Other costs borne by clients (fund expenses, custodian fees, transaction costs) are listed
- [ ] Fee negotiability and any fee waivers are addressed
- [ ] Householding/aggregation policy is stated
- [ ] Language is consistent with the advisory agreement and other client-facing documents
