# Fiduciary Analysis: Proprietary Fund Recommendation by RIA Adviser

## Short Answer

Yes, this raises a fiduciary issue. As an RIA, your firm owes a fiduciary duty under Investment Advisers Act Section 206, which includes both a duty of care and a duty of loyalty. Recommending a proprietary fund when a cheaper third-party option with similar performance exists implicates both duties.

## Applicable Standard

Your firm is a registered investment adviser, so the full federal fiduciary duty applies — not Reg BI, not suitability. This is the highest standard in the regulatory landscape. Under the SEC's 2019 Fiduciary Interpretation (Release IA-5248), this means:

- **Duty of Care:** The adviser must provide advice in the client's best interest based on a reasonable understanding of the client's objectives. For a retired teacher with moderate risk tolerance and a $600K portfolio, cost efficiency is material — every basis point of unnecessary expense directly reduces retirement income from a fixed asset base.

- **Duty of Loyalty:** The adviser must not place its own interests ahead of the client's. Recommending a proprietary fund creates a layered conflict: the firm earns both the advisory fee and revenue at the fund level. This conflict must be disclosed with sufficient specificity for the client to understand it and provide informed consent. Generic ADV language like "we may recommend affiliated products" is insufficient.

## Why This Is a Problem

### The Conflict Is Direct and Quantifiable

When an RIA recommends its own proprietary fund over a cheaper third-party alternative with comparable performance, the firm benefits from dual revenue streams — the advisory fee charged to the client and the management or distribution fees earned at the fund level. This is a textbook proprietary product conflict.

The SEC has specifically targeted this pattern. The 2018-2019 share-class selection initiative resulted in significant enforcement actions, penalties, and disgorgement orders against advisers who placed clients in higher-cost proprietary or affiliated products without adequate disclosure and justification.

### The Client Profile Amplifies the Concern

This client is a retired teacher — likely drawing on the $600K portfolio for living expenses, with limited ability to recover from unnecessary fee drag. For a moderate-risk-tolerance retiree, the cost differential between the proprietary fund and the cheaper alternative compounds directly against retirement sustainability. The duty of care requires the adviser to consider this.

### Disclosure Alone Does Not Cure the Issue

Under fiduciary duty, an adviser cannot "disclose away" a conflict. Even with full disclosure and informed consent, the adviser must still demonstrate that the recommendation is in the client's best interest despite the conflict. If the proprietary fund does not offer performance, features, or other value that justifies its higher cost relative to the third-party alternative, the recommendation may violate the duty of care regardless of how well the conflict is disclosed.

## What the Firm Must Demonstrate

To defend this recommendation, the firm needs to document all of the following:

1. **Specific disclosure of the conflict** — The client must have received clear, quantified information about the financial benefit the firm receives from the proprietary fund, the cost differential versus the third-party alternative, and the percentage of the client's portfolio allocated to proprietary products. Boilerplate ADV language is not sufficient.

2. **Informed client consent** — After receiving the specific disclosure, the client must have had the opportunity to understand the conflict and consent to the recommendation. The consent must be meaningful, not buried in onboarding paperwork.

3. **Best-interest justification** — The adviser must have a documented, reasonable basis for concluding that the proprietary fund is in this specific client's best interest despite the cost differential. This could include superior risk-adjusted performance (net of fees), specific features not available in the third-party fund (e.g., a glide path or risk management approach suited to this client's needs), or other concrete advantages. "Similar performance" at a higher cost is difficult to justify.

4. **Consideration of the client's circumstances** — The analysis must reflect this client's situation: retired, moderate risk tolerance, $600K portfolio. The cost sensitivity analysis should show the long-term impact of the fee differential on the client's retirement outcome.

## Mitigation Hierarchy

The regulatory expectation follows a clear hierarchy for addressing conflicts:

1. **Eliminate:** Stop recommending the proprietary fund when a cheaper comparable exists. This is the cleanest resolution.
2. **Mitigate:** If elimination is not chosen, implement structural safeguards — such as independent review of proprietary product recommendations, compensation leveling so the adviser has no financial incentive to favor the proprietary fund, or caps on proprietary fund allocations.
3. **Disclose:** At minimum, provide specific, quantified disclosure of the conflict and obtain informed consent. But disclosure alone is the weakest defense under fiduciary duty, and regulators have made clear it is insufficient when elimination or mitigation is practicable.

## Recommended Actions

1. **Review the specific recommendation immediately.** Determine the exact cost differential and whether the proprietary fund offers any documented advantage that could justify it for this client.
2. **Check ADV Part 2A disclosure language.** If the firm's conflict disclosure regarding proprietary products is generic or boilerplate, it needs to be revised to meet the "sufficiently specific" standard from the SEC's 2019 Interpretation.
3. **Document the rationale.** If the firm concludes the proprietary fund is justified, create a written record of the analysis — covering cost comparison, performance comparison (net of fees), and client-specific suitability factors.
4. **If the recommendation cannot be justified, reverse it.** Move the client to the lower-cost third-party option and consider whether the cost differential should be reimbursed. The SEC share-class initiative precedent involved disgorgement of the excess fees.
5. **Review firm-wide practices.** If one adviser is making this recommendation, others may be too. Conduct a broader review of proprietary product usage across client accounts.
6. **Evaluate compensation structures.** Determine whether the adviser has a financial incentive (differential compensation, revenue sharing, production credits) to recommend the proprietary fund. If so, this is an additional conflict that must be mitigated or eliminated.

## Regulatory Risk

The SEC has made proprietary product conflicts an examination and enforcement priority. The share-class selection initiative alone resulted in over 100 enforcement actions. Firms that cannot demonstrate specific disclosure, informed consent, and a documented best-interest basis for proprietary recommendations face risk of:

- SEC enforcement action under IA Act Sections 206(1) and 206(2)
- Disgorgement of excess fees charged through proprietary products
- Civil penalties
- Reputational damage and client attrition

## Bottom Line

For a retired teacher with a moderate-risk $600K portfolio, recommending a more expensive proprietary fund over a cheaper third-party alternative with similar performance is difficult to justify under fiduciary duty. The firm should treat this as a compliance matter requiring immediate review, documentation, and likely remediation. The safest path is to move the client to the lower-cost option unless the firm can produce a concrete, documented justification for the proprietary fund that goes beyond general statements about fund quality or the advisory relationship.
