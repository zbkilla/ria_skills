# Fiduciary Analysis: Proprietary Fund Recommendation

**Yes, this raises a significant fiduciary concern.**

## The Core Issue

As a Registered Investment Adviser (RIA), your firm owes clients a fiduciary duty under the Investment Advisers Act of 1940. This duty has two primary components:

1. **Duty of Care** -- the obligation to provide advice that is in the client's best interest.
2. **Duty of Loyalty** -- the obligation to put the client's interest ahead of the firm's own financial interest.

Recommending a proprietary fund over a cheaper third-party alternative with similar performance characteristics potentially violates both.

## Why This Is Problematic

**Conflict of interest.** Proprietary funds generate revenue for the firm (management fees, distribution fees, or both). When a cheaper, comparably performing alternative exists, the recommendation appears motivated by the firm's financial interest rather than the client's. This is the textbook definition of a loyalty conflict.

**Best execution / best interest.** The SEC's guidance on fiduciary duty (see the 2019 Commission Interpretation Regarding Standard of Conduct for Investment Advisers) makes clear that an adviser must seek to obtain the "best execution" for clients and cannot place its own interests ahead of the client's. Recommending a higher-cost product with no demonstrable benefit to the client fails this standard.

**Client-specific factors make it worse.** This client is:

- **Retired** -- on a fixed income, making fee drag more damaging since every basis point of unnecessary cost erodes a portfolio that is in distribution mode, not accumulation.
- **Moderate risk tolerance** -- not seeking aggressive alpha that might justify a specialty product.
- **$600K portfolio** -- a meaningful but not enormous sum where cost efficiency matters significantly to long-term outcomes.

Even a 30-50 basis point fee difference on $600K compounds to tens of thousands of dollars over a 20-30 year retirement horizon.

## Regulatory Exposure

- **SEC examination risk.** The SEC's Division of Examinations has repeatedly flagged proprietary product recommendations as a priority area. Failure to disclose and manage this conflict can result in enforcement action.
- **Form ADV disclosure.** The firm must disclose conflicts related to proprietary products in its Form ADV Part 2A (Items 6, 10, and 11). If this conflict is not clearly disclosed, that is an additional violation.
- **Reg BI (if applicable).** If any part of the recommendation involved a broker-dealer capacity, Regulation Best Interest (effective June 2020) also requires that the recommendation be in the client's best interest and that conflicts be mitigated, not merely disclosed.

## What the Firm Should Do

1. **Investigate immediately.** Determine whether the adviser had a legitimate, documentable reason for the proprietary fund recommendation (e.g., tax-loss harvesting constraints, specific exposure not available elsewhere, transition cost considerations).
2. **Review the documentation.** Check whether the adviser documented the rationale for choosing the proprietary fund. Lack of documentation is itself a compliance failure.
3. **Assess disclosure.** Confirm that the firm's Form ADV and client-facing disclosures adequately describe the conflict of interest around proprietary products.
4. **Consider remediation.** If no valid justification exists, the client should be moved to the lower-cost alternative and any excess fees incurred should be evaluated for reimbursement.
5. **Review firm-wide practices.** One instance may indicate a pattern. Audit other client accounts for similar proprietary fund placements.
6. **Document everything.** Record the review, findings, and any corrective action taken in the firm's compliance files.

## Bottom Line

Disclosure alone does not cure a fiduciary breach. An RIA cannot simply disclose a conflict and then act on it anyway if a better option exists for the client. The fiduciary standard requires that the adviser either avoid the conflict or demonstrate, with documentation, that the proprietary product is genuinely in the client's best interest despite the cost differential. On the facts presented -- similar performance, higher cost, retired client on a fixed income -- this recommendation is very difficult to justify and warrants prompt corrective action.
