# Form ADV Part 2A Fee Disclosure Guidance

Your fee structure involves three distinct compensation arrangements, each of which must be clearly disclosed in Item 5 (Fees and Compensation) of Form ADV Part 2A. Below is how to approach each.

---

## 1. Tiered AUM Fee Schedule

Present the tiered schedule in a clear table format within Item 5. The SEC and state regulators expect fee schedules to be easily understood by clients.

**Recommended disclosure format:**

| Assets Under Management | Annual Fee Rate |
|---|---|
| First $1,000,000 | 1.00% |
| Next $2,000,000 ($1M -- $3M) | 0.75% |
| Above $3,000,000 | 0.50% |

Key points to address in your narrative:

- **Billing method**: State whether fees are billed in advance or arrears, and the billing frequency (quarterly is standard). For example: "Fees are billed quarterly in advance based on the account value on the last business day of the prior quarter."
- **Fee calculation**: Clarify that the tiers are marginal (if that is your intent). A $4M account does not pay 0.50% on the full amount; it pays 1.00% on the first $1M, 0.75% on the next $2M, and 0.50% on the remaining $1M, for a blended effective rate. Provide a worked example so clients understand.
- **Proration and refunds**: Disclose how fees are handled for partial periods, new accounts opened mid-quarter, and terminated accounts. If you refund the prorated portion upon termination, state that explicitly.
- **Account aggregation (householding)**: If you allow clients to aggregate related accounts to reach higher tiers (lower rates), disclose this. If you do not, say so.
- **Fee negotiability**: If fees are negotiable, disclose that some clients may pay more or less than the published schedule and explain the circumstances. This is an SEC staff expectation.
- **Fee deduction**: State whether fees are deducted directly from client accounts or invoiced. If deducted, note that clients will receive statements from their custodian reflecting the deduction and that they should review those statements.

**Worked example to include:**

> For a client with $4,000,000 under management, the annual fee would be calculated as follows: 1.00% on the first $1,000,000 ($10,000) + 0.75% on the next $2,000,000 ($15,000) + 0.50% on the remaining $1,000,000 ($5,000) = $30,000 annually, or $7,500 per quarter. This represents a blended annual rate of 0.75%.

---

## 2. Fixed Financial Planning Fee

The $2,500 annual financial planning fee must be disclosed separately from the AUM fee. Address the following:

- **Scope of service**: Describe what the financial planning fee covers (comprehensive plan, ongoing updates, specific deliverables) so clients understand what they are paying for.
- **Relationship to AUM fee**: Clarify whether the financial planning fee is charged in addition to the AUM fee, or whether it applies only to clients who do not have AUM accounts. If it is additive, state that plainly.
- **Billing terms**: When is the fee charged (upon engagement, annually on a specific date, upon plan delivery)? Is it paid in advance or arrears?
- **Refund policy**: If a client terminates mid-engagement, is any portion refundable? The SEC's rules on prepayment of fees exceeding $1,200 more than six months in advance (under Rule 205-3 and custody rule considerations) may be relevant depending on your billing structure.
- **Whether the fee is negotiable**: As with AUM fees, disclose if the amount can vary.

---

## 3. 12b-1 Fee Disclosure -- This Is the Most Sensitive Area

Receiving 12b-1 fees from mutual funds held in client accounts creates a **conflict of interest** that demands careful, prominent disclosure. The SEC has brought enforcement actions against advisers who failed to adequately disclose these arrangements. Key requirements:

### Item 5 Disclosure

- **State that you receive 12b-1 fees**: Explain that certain mutual fund share classes pay trail commissions (12b-1 fees) to your firm, and that this is compensation in addition to the advisory fee.
- **Quantify or characterize the amount**: If possible, provide a range or typical percentage (e.g., "typically 0.25% per year of fund assets"). If the amounts vary, say so.
- **Explain the conflict**: The conflict is that you have a financial incentive to recommend or retain mutual fund share classes that pay 12b-1 fees over share classes of the same fund that do not (e.g., institutional shares or ETFs). You must state this directly.

### Item 10 (Other Financial Industry Activities and Affiliations) and Item 14 (Client Referrals and Other Compensation)

- Cross-reference the 12b-1 fee disclosure in these sections as well. Item 14 specifically requires disclosure of economic benefits you receive from non-clients (the fund companies) in connection with providing advisory services.

### Share Class Selection Disclosure

This is where many advisers have gotten into trouble. The SEC's Share Class Selection Disclosure Initiative (2018) resulted in over 100 enforcement actions against advisers who placed clients in higher-cost share classes that paid 12b-1 fees when lower-cost share classes of the same fund were available.

You must disclose:

- Whether lower-cost share classes are available for the same funds.
- Why you use the share classes you do (if there is a legitimate reason beyond the 12b-1 fee, state it).
- What steps you take to mitigate the conflict (e.g., crediting 12b-1 fees back to client accounts, offsetting them against advisory fees, or transitioning to lower-cost share classes).

### Best Practice Recommendations

- **Credit 12b-1 fees back to clients or offset against advisory fees.** If you do this, disclose it prominently -- it significantly mitigates the conflict.
- **If you do not offset**, explain why, and be prepared for examiner scrutiny. State clearly that the 12b-1 fee is additional cost borne by the client through the fund's expense ratio, on top of your advisory fee.
- **Consider transitioning to institutional or clean share classes** that do not pay 12b-1 fees. This eliminates the conflict entirely and is the direction the industry has moved post-DOL fiduciary rule and SEC Reg BI.

## 4. Additional Disclosure Considerations

### Total Cost to Client

Consider including a summary that helps the client understand total all-in costs. For example:

> "A client with $2,000,000 under management who also engages us for financial planning would pay approximately $17,500 in advisory fees ($10,000 + $7,500) plus $2,500 in financial planning fees, totaling $20,000 annually, or approximately 1.00% of assets. Clients may also bear mutual fund expense ratios, which typically range from 0.05% to 0.75% depending on the fund, and a portion of those expenses may be paid to us as 12b-1 fees."

### Other Costs Not Charged by You

Item 5 also requires disclosure of other costs clients will bear that are not part of your fee: custodian transaction charges, mutual fund expense ratios, wire transfer fees, and similar. Briefly list these so the client understands total cost of ownership.

### Wrap Fee Disclosure

If your program could be characterized as a wrap fee program (bundling advisory fees and transaction costs), you may need to complete Schedule H (Wrap Fee Program Brochure) instead of or in addition to the standard Part 2A.

---

## 5. Regulatory References

- **SEC Form ADV Part 2A, Item 5** (Fees and Compensation) -- primary disclosure location
- **SEC Form ADV Part 2A, Item 10** (Other Financial Industry Activities) -- 12b-1 conflict cross-reference
- **SEC Form ADV Part 2A, Item 14** (Client Referrals and Other Compensation) -- 12b-1 as third-party compensation
- **SEC Investment Advisers Act Rule 204-3** (delivery of brochure requirements)
- **SEC Share Class Selection Disclosure Initiative (2018)** -- precedent for 12b-1 enforcement
- **SEC Regulation Best Interest (Reg BI)** -- if you have dual-registered representatives, the 12b-1 conflict triggers Reg BI obligations on the broker-dealer side as well
